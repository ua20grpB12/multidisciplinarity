Newspapers in Bubblewrap, Belgium

Newspapers in Bubblewrap by Denmark

The art of Denmark consists of printed media (information carriers), which he uses to create his objects or installations. In doing so, he aims to express his feeling of impotence against the abundance of information that this era brings with it. His installations are created through transformation, destruction and reconstruction of newspapers, magazines and books in all kinds of forms. The artist uses a mix of different techniques that can be categorized under conservation and destruction. For example, stacking, pressing, cutting, sealing, gluing, binding and many more. Often, he performs these techniques in a repetitive manner, so that a ritual arises that confronts his craft with industrial production. Often also to encourage reflection.
Newspapers in Bubblewrap is a temporary installation exhibited in 2009. Denmark used the technique of “sealing” the information. As the name of the installation already suggests, it is an installation in which information carriers in the form of newspapers are wrapped in bubble plastic. This creates what looks kind of like columnar rows of bubble plastic. Again, a statement against the information age that we live in. The huge amount of information and the artists feeling of importance against it. This project has a minimal multidisciplinary nature. It can be seen as assembly art, installation art, sculptural art, etcetera. But beyond that, no real different disciplines are being combined here. 

Monochrome Till Receipt, London

Monochrome Till Receipt by Ceal Floyer

Typical for Ceal Floyer is that she transforms everyday, normally overlooked objects into art. She does this by adjusting something small or simply by placing them in a different context, so that we suddenly look at these objects differently. She tests the difference between function and implication, the literal and the imagined. Her interventions increase our awareness of the environment, as it were.
Monochrome Till Receipt (1999) is a receipt from the Morrisons supermarket. At first glance, this just looks like a till receipt like any other, but if you take a closer look at the ticket you will see that it only contains white products. Flour, sugar, white chocolate, ... In total there are 49 products on the till receipt, all white. Can you imagine? A shopping cart completely filled with only white products. Normally, a till receipt which you receive from the supermarket, is simply a proof of purchase. By giving it a different interpretation, she makes us look at it differently and think more deeply about it. Thus becoming art.
